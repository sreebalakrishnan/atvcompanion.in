step 3 · how this is computed
How does a date, a time, and a city become a sidereal Lagna? Eight steps of celestial mechanics, computed entirely in your browser — no server, no API, no cached predictions.

Everything in the chart above is computed from first principles — birth time, location, and the laws of celestial mechanics. Here is the full pipeline, in the order the code runs.

01

Local civil time → UTC

Birth time is entered in the local clock time at the place of birth. We convert to UTC using the location's IANA timezone (e.g. Asia/Kolkata, America/New_York) through the browser's Intl.DateTimeFormat API. This honours historical DST transitions: a Bangalore birth before 1945 will see a different offset than after; a New York birth in July uses EDT, in January EST. The actual offset applied is shown beneath every output.

// Two-pass iteration to converge through DST seams
dtf  = Intl.DateTimeFormat('en-US', { timeZone: 'Asia/Kolkata', … })
off1 = offsetMinutes( assumedUtc )
real = assumedUtc − off1
off2 = offsetMinutes( real )
if ( off2 ≠ off1 ) real = assumedUtc − off2
02

UTC → Julian Date

The Julian Date (JD) counts days continuously from 1 January 4713 BCE — the universal currency of positional astronomy. The reference epoch J2000.0 is JD 2451545.0 (1 Jan 2000, 12:00 UTC). All ephemeris polynomials are expressed in centuries from this epoch.

aTime = Astronomy.MakeTime( utcDate )
JD    = aTime.tt + 2451545.0    // tt = days since J2000.0
T     = (JD − 2451545.0) / 36525  // Julian centuries
03

Planetary positions — geocentric ecliptic

The seven classical graha — Sūrya, Chandra, Maṅgala, Budha, Guru, Śukra, Śani — are computed by astronomy-engine v2.1.19, a JavaScript port of the IAU-recommended VSOP87 planetary theory (Bretagnon & Francou, 1988) and ELP-2000/82 lunar theory (Chapront-Touzé & Chapront, 1983). Accuracy: better than 1 arcminute for our era — well below sign-boundary precision. Positions are returned in the J2000 ecliptic frame (the mean ecliptic of 1 Jan 2000).

for body in [Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn]:
   vec   = Astronomy.GeoVector( body, aTime, false )
   ecl   = Astronomy.Ecliptic( vec )
   λ_tropical = ecl.elon              // ecliptic longitude, J2000
04

Tropical → Sidereal (Lahiri ayanamsha)

Vedic astrology is sidereal — measured against the fixed stars, not the moving vernal equinox. The two zodiacs drift apart by roughly 50.29 arcseconds per year due to the precession of the equinoxes. The Lahiri ayanamsha — adopted by the Government of India's Rashtriya Panchang Reform Committee in 1955 — is the standard offset, with the equinoxes coinciding in 285 CE. At J2000.0 it equals 23.853°.

// Linear drift model — adequate to ~0.01° over a century
ayanamsha(JD) = 23.853 + (JD − 2451545.0) / 365.25 × (50.29 / 3600)
λ_sidereal    = ( λ_tropical − ayanamsha ) mod 360

Since astronomy-engine returns J2000-frame coordinates, planets use the constant 23.853° directly. The Lagna and the lunar nodes — computed in the equator/ecliptic of date — use the drifted value ayanamsha(JD).

05

Lagna — the rising sign

The Lagna is the point of the ecliptic intersecting the eastern horizon at birth. It needs three inputs: Local Sidereal Time (LST — the right ascension currently transiting the meridian, also written RAMC), the obliquity of the ecliptic (ε ≈ 23.4393°), and the geographic latitude (φ).

// 5.1 — Greenwich Mean Sidereal Time (Meeus, simplified)
GMST = 280.46061837
     + 360.98564736629 × (JD − 2451545.0)
     + 0.000387933 × T²
     − T³ / 38710000

// 5.2 — Local Sidereal Time (≡ RAMC)
LST  = ( GMST + longitude ) mod 360

// 5.3 — Tropical Ascendant, of date
λ_asc = atan2( −cos(LST),
               sin(ε)·tan(φ) + cos(ε)·sin(LST) )

// 5.4 — Tropical Mid-Heaven, of date
λ_mc  = atan2( sin(LST), cos(LST)·cos(ε) )

// 5.5 — Quadrant fix — atan2 may return the Descendant.
//       Asc must lie east of MC: (Asc − MC) mod 360 ∈ (0°, 180°)
if ( (λ_asc − λ_mc + 360) mod 360 > 180 )
    λ_asc = (λ_asc + 180) mod 360

// 5.6 — Subtract Lahiri-of-date for the sidereal Lagna
λ_lagna = ( λ_asc − ayanamsha(JD) ) mod 360

Step 5.5 was the bug fixed in v4.74.24 — the inequality was reversed, which silently put every chart's Lagna on its Descendant. We now verify against published charts (deva.guru, jhora) as part of regression.

06

Rāhu & Ketu — the lunar nodes

The two remaining Navagraha are not physical bodies — they are the intersection points of the Moon's orbit with the ecliptic. The ascending node is Rāhu; the descending node, Ketu, is exactly 180° opposite. Vedic tradition (Vimśottari daśā in particular) uses the mean node — a smooth orbital average — rather than the true (osculating) node, which oscillates by ±1.5° on a 173-day cycle.

// Mean ascending node of the Moon (Meeus 47.7), tropical
Ω = 125.04452
   − 1934.136261 × T
   + 0.0020708   × T²
   + T³ / 450000

λ_rahu_tropical = Ω mod 360
λ_rahu_sidereal = ( λ_rahu_tropical − ayanamsha(JD) ) mod 360
λ_ketu_sidereal = ( λ_rahu_sidereal + 180 ) mod 360

The node retrogrades through the zodiac — Ω decreases at ~19.34° per year — making one full circuit in roughly 18.6 years. This is the rhythm of the eclipse seasons.

07

Sign & degree

The sidereal zodiac is divided into twelve 30° arcs starting from Meṣa (sidereal Aries) at 0°. The signIndex is a simple floor; the degree-within-sign is the remainder.

signIndex = ⌊ λ_sidereal / 30 ⌋     // 0 = Aries … 11 = Pisces
degInSign = λ_sidereal − signIndex × 30
08

Counting the signature

For each of the ten placements (9 Navagraha + Lagna) we read the Element, Modality, and Duality of the sign it occupies. We tally three tables: {Fire, Earth, Air, Water}, {Cardinal, Fixed, Mutable}, {Yang, Yin}. The dominant in each table is the highest count; ties are reported as ties.

Per Vikram's rule, the counted Duality is informational only — the duality that defines the signature is derived from the dominant Element: Fire and Air are always Yang; Earth and Water are always Yin. The final signature is the zodiac sign whose (Element × Modality) combination matches the dominant pair — for example, dominant Earth × dominant Cardinal yields Capricorn, regardless of whether the counted Yin was a few above or below the counted Yang.

Sources & honest limitations. Ephemerides: VSOP87 + ELP-2000/82 via astronomy-engine. Sidereal time, Ascendant, MC, and lunar-node formulae: Meeus, Astronomical Algorithms, 2nd ed., chapters 12, 22, 47. Ayanamsha: Lahiri (Calendar Reform Committee, 1955) with linear drift — adequate to ~0.01° over a century. What this does not include: nutation, aberration, proper motion of stars, topocentric parallax for the Moon, true (osculating) node. For the Element/Modality/Duality signature these omissions are well below the noise floor; for high-precision muhūrta or rectification, use a dedicated ephemeris such as Swiss Ephemeris.
