Part 1 — General Course Guidelines

These guidelines were stated by the teacher (Vikram) across multiple sessions and apply throughout the entire year-long course.

1.1  The Core Principle: Your Own Birth Chart Only

The foundational rule of Year 1 is to work exclusively with your own birth chart. The teacher was emphatic about this across many sessions:

A written promise (sent by email) was required from each student pledging not to read the birth charts of other people during this year. The teacher made clear this is a formal commitment — as a student to a teacher, not just a casual agreement.

1.2  Journal of Insights — Ongoing Throughout the Year

Keeping a Journal of Insights is the primary ongoing task for the entire year. Mentioned in the Intro Call and reiterated in many subsequent sessions:

Maintain a journal where you record your reflections and insights from each session.

Map what you learn (mythology, astronomy, philosophy) to your own birth chart.

Show the teacher how you are forming connections: e.g., "Saturn in my chart relates to the Kurma Avatar story because..."

The teacher wants to check you are making the right links — not wrong associations between stories and planets.

Journaling is optional to share, but highly encouraged. It is the foundation for chart interpretation.

The journal continues and deepens into Year 2 and beyond.

"So the goal here is to maintain a journal of your insights. And what I would like to see... is the mapping between your insights about who you are and what you are seeing in your birth chart." — Session 8

1.3  Assignment Submission Format (Updated mid-course)

From approximately Session 36 onward, the teacher updated the homework submission method. The new format applies to all chart-interpretation assignments:

Create a single Google Document. Title it exactly: 101 - [Your Full Name]

At the top, include a 'Birth Details' heading with your date, time, and place of birth.

Insert a screenshot of your birth chart (South Indian format, D1) into the document.

Use headings for each section (e.g., 'Interpreting the Ascendant', 'Second House'). These become navigation tabs automatically.

Write your interpretations under each heading.

All future assignments are appended to the same document — do not create a new document each time.

Highlight or indicate which section you have recently completed so the teacher can find it easily.

Share the document with the teacher — they will receive email notifications when you edit it.

1.4  How the Course Approaches Assignments

The teacher explained that this course is fundamentally different from academic coursework:

Assignments are living documents — you keep adding to them as your understanding deepens.

There are no hard deadlines. Submit whenever you can. The teacher checks whenever you give access.

If you are struggling with self-study or self-reflection, reach out for a one-on-one session.

If you are struggling with astrology software or technology, contact the teacher directly.

1.5  Reflection vs. Action — How to Use the Prompts

Many assignments involve reflection and journaling rather than technical calculation. The teacher gave this guidance:

When given a list of reflection prompts, do NOT try to do them all — you will get overwhelmed.

Pick any one prompt that stands out for you and go deep into it.

Reflective prompts are meant for journaling, meditation, introspection — not for producing a formal answer.

"The more you do these kinds of reflections, the easier it will be for you to interpret your chart." (Session 9)

Two types of journaling described: (1) Morning Pages style — write non-stop for a set time; (2) Jungian journaling — write to one specific question.

1.6  Reading Other People's Charts — Progression

The teacher outlined the full 4-year roadmap. This matters because it clarifies what 'assignments' look like in subsequent years:

Year 1 (Foundations): Work only with your own birth chart. Build the Journal of Insights.

Year 2 (Beginners): Interpret the teacher's chart — 3 sessions of 1 hour each. Receive feedback after each session.

Year 3 (Intermediate): Read charts of family/friends/clients, but first discuss each chart with a mentor. Aim for 7–10 clients.

Year 4 (Advanced): Independent readings. Monthly group reflections. Journal of learnings continues.

Session 2 — Homework: The Listening/Writing Practice

The teacher gave a specific contemplative writing practice as homework for the week ahead:

Assignment: Non-Stop Writing Practice

Take 4 sheets of paper and a pen with full ink.

Eliminate all distractions — phone on silent, doors closed, A/C at the right temperature.

Sit down and start writing non-stop, without lifting your pen from the paper.

The theme: 'Listen to that which is not human' — write without judgment or censorship.

Continue for at least 30 minutes (you do not need to stop at 30 minutes if it is flowing).

The teacher also mentioned that dream journaling is a different but related practice (introduced later in the course, particularly around the water element sessions).

Session 17 — Jungian Journaling Introduction

The teacher introduced Jungian journaling as a specific practice:

Jungian Journaling (Indian style):

Session 24 — Major Exercise: Dispositor Chain Across 12 Charts

This was a major practical exercise done in breakout rooms. It is one of the core technical assignments of the course:

The Task:

Choose ONE planet from: Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn, Rahu, or Ketu. Write it down in your journal.

The teacher provided 12 sample charts. For your chosen planet, work through ALL 12 charts.

For each chart, follow this process:

Find which sign your chosen planet is placed in.

Is the planet exalted, debilitated, or in a neutral/own sign?

Find the dispositor: who is the ruler/lord of the sign your planet is in?

Where is the dispositor placed? In which sign?

Is the dispositor exalted, debilitated, or in a neutral/own sign?

Repeat this chain: find the dispositor of the dispositor, and so on.

STOP when: (a) you reach a planet that is in its own sign (it is its own dispositor), or (b) you find a loop (a planet has already appeared in the chain).

Identify the FINAL DISPOSITOR — the planet at the end of the chain.

Record the final dispositor for your planet in each of the 12 charts.

Work in your breakout room pair/trio. You can work individually and then cross-check, or work through charts together if you have chosen the same planet.

Session 34 — Assignment: Interpretive Sentences for Your Own Chart

This was the main formal assignment introduced around Session 33–34, involving writing actual interpretive sentences for your birth chart. This is the assignment that was reviewed and returned with comments in Session 35.

The Assignment:

Open your birth chart (South Indian D1 format). Take a screenshot to include in your Google Doc.

Start with the Ascendant (first house). Write TWO sentences:

Sentence 1 — The Technical Sentence: State the sign, its element, modality, and duality. e.g., 'My ascendant is Virgo — Mutable Earth Yin.'

Sentence 2 — The Interpretation: Translate the technical facts into a statement about yourself. e.g., 'I approach life with adaptability and groundedness, seeking to serve and refine.'

Repeat for each house — write both the technical sentence and an interpretive sentence.

In your document, mark what you are combining with letters (e.g., 'A + B') so the teacher can see your process. You CANNOT combine two qualities from the same category (A + A is not permitted).

Worked Examples from Class (Session 34/35):

"My ascendant is sacred — [sign quality] — responsible for my health."

"[Sign rising]: lord of ascendant is in the 4th house. I start with my family as stable ground."

"I am empathetic and flexible." (Mutable + Earth + Yin combined)

"Passive investments over the long run." (Capricorn in the second house — Saturn, Earth, Fixed)

Session 35 — Assignment Review & General Pointers

Session 35 was largely dedicated to reviewing the interpretive sentences assignment. Key points from the teacher:

The teacher had commented on ALL assignments before the session — open your document and review comments before the session.

Breakout rooms were used: students shared what they had done with a colleague (15 minutes), looking at how different people made sentences for the same chart.

It is fine — and good — to write a full paragraph rather than a single sentence.

"Great work" was given to students who wrote longer interpretive paragraphs.

When stuck: look at other students' sentences as examples and see how they built them.

The process matters: always show which building blocks (letters) you combined.

Houses Assignment — Ongoing

As you work through each house, the teacher gives worked examples in class of how to form interpretive sentences for houses. Key points for homework:

For each house, identify the sign on the cusp of that house in your chart.

Apply the letters system: combine one quality from the sign (element, modality, duality) with one quality from the house signification.

You CANNOT take two words from the same category (e.g., two elements, or two modalities).

Write the sentence, indicate the letters, and show your working.

Example: Capricorn (Saturn, Earth, Cardinal, Yin) on the second house (wealth, finances) → 'I build wealth through disciplined, structured, long-term effort.'
